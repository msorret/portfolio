The App Academy

The App Academy is a four year computer science pathway at Pasadena High School, funded by the Pasadena Educational Foundation. The program has a total of 200 students and 3 teachers, including myself.

Take a look inside my classroom!

My Role

I wear many hats in this position! I am the lead teacher, curriculum designer, and program manager. Basically, I am the point person for the program, managing anything necessary to keep it running.

I am responsible for:

  • Designing the curriculum for all four grade levels - syllabi, course overviews, learning standards, unit maps, projects, assessments, and rubrics for 4 year long courses in topics using industry level frameworks and software.

  • Working with district administration and counselors to manage course scheduling, cohorting, Career Technical Education and Linked Learning requirements
  • Organizing program events such as hackathons, field trips, guest speakers/mentors, and community based events
  • Working with PEF, donors, and partners to organize partnerships and manage program budget
  • Conducting presentations to board members and stakeholders

  • And my favorite part - teaching courses for 9-12th grade students!


Curriculum Design

Over the past four years, I have designed, implemented, tested and taught four computer science courses for the high school level. The curriculum I have designed has been implemented by myself, as well as three other computer science teachers.

Introduction to JavaScript using p5.js

Students learn the basics of coding in JavaScript by building fun projects like Battleship, Fruit Ninja, Codenames, etc. By the end of the course, they will have covered variables, conditionals, loops, functions, data structures, and object oriented programming.

Full Stack Web Development // Video Game Development

Students may choose either a web or game development course. Web Dev students learn how build a website using front-end frame work React, as well as build and use their own APIs in the backend. Game Dev students build 2-D or 3-D games using the game engine Godot.

AP Computer Science Principles

Students prepare for the AP CS Principles exam by covering topics in algorithms, data science, machine learning, networking, & more - switching over to the language Python! On average, 80% of App students pass the exam each year, earning college credit.

Senior Portfolio

Students complete self-paced long term projects in video game development, virtual reality, mobile app development, and data science, as they build their own portfolio website to showcase their work and skills!



Data Driven Design Model

I want the curriculum I build to be designed with the students in my classroom in mind. I am constantly surveying and pulling data to make sure that projects, assessments, units, and overall courses are working effectively.

After each project and assessment, students complete a short survey to find out how difficult they found the assignment, how much they enjoyed it, how much time it took, and how prepared they felt to complete it.


This data allows me to adjust in real time. If a significant portion of my students felt unprepared or did not enjoy a project, I can adjust the course schedule, go back and reteach certain topics, or develop the next project in a different style/subject.


This data also allows me to work effectively with my team - the other teachers implementing this curriculum. We meet regularly to discuss these data points and make changes to the curriculum or instructional design/resources.


This survey data is especially useful when used in combination with student grades and performance data. These surveys allowed me to get insight into why a student may not be performing well in the class. These surveys allowed me to get insight into why a student may not be performing well in the class or identify students who need to be challenged more.


All students in the App Academy are also surveyed at the end of the first month, the first semester, and the end of the year. In these surveys, I learn a lot more about student's thoughts and feelings about the class and program overall. Their answers help me adjust the overall design of the course. I learn about topics they wished we spent more time on, topics they wished we spent less, times of the year they were more or less productive, topics they wish we had covered.



And I use a lot of their suggestions! For example, in the 2022-23 academic year, the sophomore course was just a web development course. In a student survey at the end of the semester, a student expressed he wished that we had covered principles of UX design, research, and the development process in other contexts, such as game development. His response was so compelling that I refactored the second semester curriculum to split between web and game development. This year, that change has remained and become a permanent part of the course pathway.


Interdisciplinary Design

Coming from a highly interdisciplinary background, I knew I wanted to create a curriculum that was diverse in it's design and catered to students with all sorts of different interests and goals.

To help students explore their interests and connect with other like-minded students, I created the marble class system.

The marble classes are modeled after the Harry Potter houses. But instead of personality traits, they are based off of intersections of technology and other fields. The marble classes named after four notable people in the intersection of technology. Students are sorted into one of the four classes based on their own interests in technology.


Students complete projects, class activities, and games within their marble classes. Through these activities, they can also earn marbles for their class. When a marble class earns 200 marbles, they throw a marble party at lunch - inviting members of the class across all four grades. This has been a great way for the students to build relationships in other grades, meet students with similar interests, and expand on class projects in ways that are meaningful to them.


I tested this idea with my own classes in the 2022-23 academic year, then expanded it to the entire program in the 2023-24 - making adjustments based on student feedback. Over the summer of 2023, I also redecorated four classrooms to make common rooms & project spaces for the four marble classes. I've also organized marble themed events such as a hackathon, computer science olympics, encryption escape rooms, and field trips to tech companies, museums, and college campuses.

Fun Fridays

Fun Fridays was an idea I started my first year, as a way for students to take a bi-monthly mental health break and bond with their classmates. Every other week, students participate in Fun Friday - an off screen class period where we'll play games, do class activities, make art, or go out to the field to play sports. I design many of these class activities and games myself - some for Fun Fridays, some for class reviews, and some to act as class bonding activities.

Fun Fridays have been adopted by all the teachers in the App Academy program, as well other teachers on campus. I have distributed a number of these self-made class games and ice breakers to other teachers.

I have detailed a few of my favorite class games that I've designed myself!



Coding Survivor

Based off of the popular show Survivor. students are divided into tribes of programming languages. Each student is allowed to pick a coding structure or library in their language. They can pick something as simple as loops or as complicated as the numpy library. There are allowed to be duplicates, although it is at their disadvantage.
In each round, students participate in a reward and immunity challenge. Reward challenges are fun and non-coding related - and the winning team might get candy, a clue to the hidden immunity idol, or an advantage. In immunity challenges, teams are giving a coding challenge - which they must complete using only the coding structures or libraries they have in their team. The team that loses the challenge must go to Tribal Council and vote one of their coding structures out. The voted out member becomes part of the jury.

When half the members are voted out, the remaining players will merge. At the merge, the remaining players will draft the coding structures that were previously voted out of the game. Those will make up what each player will have moving forward into the individual portion. This forces the players to balance the team and individual portions of the game. If you vote out useful structures, the team portion is much more difficult, but if you make it to the merge, you will have better options for individual portion.

During this merge, the jury works on coming up with a final coding challenge - trying to make it as tricky as possible. When three players remain, they will compete using the jury’s coding challenge. The winner earns 50 marbles for their class. 2nd place earns 20 and 3rd place earns 10. Everyone who makes the merge earns 1 marble each.

When I get inspired, I will create advantages and hidden immunity idols that I hide around the classroom in the days leading up to the next round. The game is played over the course of a semester, usually running one round each week.


Secret Grinch

Based loosely on Mafia, Secret Grinch is a rendition of a lying-based coop game. In this game there are two teams - the villains and the heroes. The villains are made up of the Grinch and his dog Max. The heroes are Who characters.

The goal of the heroes is to award everyone a gift. The goal of the villains is to have everyone on their team receive a gift and vote the Grinch as Santa Claus.

In each round, someone is nominated and voted as Santa. Santa picks up two cards from the deck. The deck is made up of cards face down, each of which have either a present or a piece of coal. Sometimes you might get two presents, two pieces of coal, or one of each. Santa must then discard one without showing the group and give the other to one of the other players.

If a player receives a piece of coal, it eliminates any gifts they might have at that point. If they receive three pieces of coal, they are voted out of the game. The Whos must gift everyone still in the game a present OR vote the Grinch out to win. The Grinch and Max must have a present and the Grinch must be voted in as Santa to win.

I have desinged multiple renditions such as Secret Voldemort: Death Eaters vs. Dumbledore’s Army and Secret Leperchaun: Leperchauns vs. Kingergartners. It’s a fun game of strategy, deceit, and trust! Many classes around campus have used this game as a fun icebreaker activity!
